# Rebuilding the internet

I have been thinking about how boring the current internet has become. Everything is an algorithm; everything is trying to steal your time, make you feel worse, and take the last penny from you. You know the drill. But it wasn't always this way. The internet used to be a haven. So I began to think: if we had to rebuild the "good" internet from scratch, what would the fundamental rules be? Here are a few suggestions.

These ideas are directly influenced by my use of the internet, especially social media. It has a lot of good use cases, but now more than ever it has more negatives. Many of these are intentional problems manufactured by companies to drive profit while negatively affecting users. A lot of this can be avoided.

The main goal of this essay is to show how, if we rebuilt social media, it could be better than the slop we have created.

## No Monetization of Thoughts

Monetization of content has existed since before I was born, but its current state is bad; most opinions are sponsored in favor of something. The first thing I would do is stop monetizing creators through ads. This single-handedly drives creators to post engagement bait to maximize views. Removing it would force creators to focus on good content instead.

To fix the loss of revenue, creators could display their products on their pages or take direct payment from fans. This would incentivize creators to make good content and good products. Ads may still exist to support the infrastructure of this "new web," but they will never directly pay creators.

This would also make content more authentic. Most creators today hire large teams to cater to a large audience; without view-based earnings, smaller and more authentic creators can thrive, and big labels will play a smaller role in how content is made.

## Stricter Moderation

The problem with today's internet, especially a certain social media platform (X), is that it has spread a lot of misinformation. Elon's claim that a lack of moderation supports free speech has only made Twitter worse. Using AI to moderate may be a good decision, but a human should always have the final call. Hate speech will not be tolerated under any circumstances. Well-defined rules will determine what does and does not count as hate speech. A safer internet is a better internet for everyone.

Hate has become too normalized in the modern era, and it is often rewarded with engagement, which defeats the purpose of a social platform. Engagement bait will be struck down. Blatant propaganda and fake news accounts will be strictly punished.

AI makes it easier than ever to stop this kind of misinformation, making the internet a better place to share thoughts. Tools like community notes, where users can directly counter misinformation, will help fight fake news. This reduces the damage done late responses of the moderation team and gives users the tools to fight back against misinformation.

## The New Currency of Popularity

Currently, popularity on social media is measured by views and likes, which leads to a huge amount of botted engagement-bait content. The new internet will treat comments as a genuine form of interaction and the new measure of popularity. This isn't foolproof, but it increases the friction required to interact, so viewers must actively engage with a creator to support them.

Second, views, likes, and follower counts will be hidden, since they directly affect how content is perceived. Content should be judged on what it is, not on what others think of it. This will lead to more original thought, unaffected by majority opinion.

This will kill a large share of manufactured hype, and creators who engage with their community will be rewarded.

## Cares for Your Time

I dislike wasting hours mindlessly doomscrolling. It has been shown to negatively affect the brain and mental health. Entering social media should require some friction, so it takes intentional effort to use. Time on the platform should also be limited. Cutting off access entirely might be the optimal approach, but gentle reminders of real life may work better.

## No More Short-Form Content or Predatory Algorithms

Short-form content has always been a net negative. We need to move past cheap tactics that harm us just to increase revenue, and instead focus on improving our lives. Long-form content will be given higher preference.

Predatory algorithms will be eliminated and replaced with a much simpler algorithm that recommends content users prefer, without making the platform addictive.

Friction will be added to make long sessions more difficult. This will likely reduce the number of users, but appealing to the masses would only turn the platform into more of the same.

## More Human Content

Content from creators and brands won't be heavily pushed. Instead, content from friends and social circles will take priority, making the experience more authentic and closer to the original meaning of social media. This will make people more mindful of what they share, rather than posting for the masses. AI-generated art will be strictly labeled, and anyone who passes it off as original art will face serious consequences. Something without a soul cannot be treated the same as human art.

## Negativity Is Given Less Value

A large part of social media algorithms push negative content to drive engagement. To reduce this, direct hate will be hidden or pushed down the feed, leading to a safer internet. Criticism, however, will not be treated the same as hate. Criticism needs to exist in a balanced way so everyone can grow and improve.

## Boring by Default

The goal is a platform that isn't addictive but is still usable. Users will have options to customize their experience, but by default the platform will be boring, so no one is pulled into a doomscrolling loop.

Users will be able to customize their homepage and how content is served to them. A good starting point is making the platform a reliable place for news and factual information.

## The Organization Is Non-Profit

A non-profit structure means the platform can never push bad choices for the sake of profitability. People come first. Chasing popularity often forces organizations to abandon their core values to appeal to the masses, as happened with companies like Google ("Don't be evil"). This can be addressed with a strict pay cap, so leadership cannot earn more even as revenue grows, with a large share of profit reinvested into the platform.

Being niche doesn't mean being a loss making company. Kagi, a browser, has fewer than 100,000 users but is still profitable. User feedback will remain at the heart of the organization.


## Conclusion

Social media wasn't flawed; it was made into this addictive, evil slop machine by companies with their own interests in mind. If a new internet is ever born, it might not beat the existing ones, but it will definitely have a soul.
