# Lessons from Hackathons

## Introduction

I have participated in more than 5 hackathons with a lot to learn from each one. It's been a pleasant experience and, with no plans of stopping, I decided to reflect on the things I learnt.

## Adaptability

Adapting quickly to change is necessary at hackathons. The easiest way to lose is to have a rigid plan you refuse to change. During my first hackathon I was set on the idea that only manual coding was allowed and vibe coding was a sin. Only on reaching there did I realize not one participant actually coded manually. Every line was vibe coded. If I did not adapt, we would not have won.

Sometimes your idea won't go as planned. You have to accept it and make the change quickly. Time is short, and time taken to resist change is time wasted.

## Idea & Implementation

The biggest wrong idea I had was that the idea of a project is greater than its implementation. I was wrong. In hackathons, implementation matters more than the idea. A simple project with good execution will beat a project with an innovative idea but poor execution. Judges evaluate the final product for what it is, not what it could be.

This taught me not to overthink the idea and instead focus on giving the best execution. Spending more time on implementation meant brainstorming a solvable idea quickly and building a functional MVP within the time frame.

## Vibe Coding

For a long time I had a clear stance on vibe coding: don't use it. There are clear reasons: tech debt and cognitive offloading, to name two. I went to my first hackathon fully committed to not vibe coding at all. I was proven wrong. Everyone vibe codes at hackathons because judges expect an MVP, not a full product. Participants are incentivized to vibe code since it helps develop features faster, and small bugs and security issues can be ignored in an MVP.

## Planning

Most of my plans during hackathons rarely went as planned. During my first hackathon I had imagined an entirely different team. Luckily it didn't work out, because now I have a better one. Sometimes plans don't go as intended, and that's for the best. You have to adapt and adjust quickly.

The second thing about planning is project planning. When I started, I didn't take it seriously, I wasn't willing to spend more than half an hour on it. My teammates weren't on the same page; they spent one or two hours discussing the project. That deep planning gave clarity to the direction we should be heading and cleared up a lot of misunderstandings about the project.

## Communication

The better you communicate, the more likely you are to win. This has two parts. The first is during the presentation. You should be able to clearly communicate your idea to the judges, show them the vision you see in your project, and make them understand why you deserve to win. If you can't communicate well, you're likely to lose even with a good project.

The second is team communication. Your team should understand your work and the reasoning behind it. Better communication has single-handedly improved the speed of our progress. One way we improved it was by sharing a doc everyone had access to, where we laid down the ground rules of the project so we were all on the same page, literally! (pun intended)

## Conclusion

I would recommend anyone from a computer engineering background to participate in hackathons. There are a lot of lessons to learn, and I still have more hackathons ahead of me. Hopefully this article gave you an insight into how hackathons work.
